Homeless Population in New York City

Homelessness in New York City has reached the highest levels since the Great Depression of the 1930s and is now 56 percent higher than it was ten years ago. At its peak, there were over 63,000 homeless.

The primary cause of homelessness, especially among families, is lack of affordable housing. Some other causes include: eviction, overcrowded housing, domestic violence, job loss, and hazardous housing conditions.

The homeless population ticked down in May 2019 for the first time since 2016 after the city invested $2 billion in homelessness prevention by expanding access to rent arrears grants and free legal services for tenants facing eviction.

“We would have more than 70,000 people in shelter today if it wasn’t for prevention and housing investments,” Human Resources Administration Commissioner Steve Banks said, after a City Council budget hearing in 2019.

The eviction moratorium, which was extended through Jan. 2021, has prevented a surge in homelessness due to the pandemic. But there is a looming eviction crisis after Jan. 1, with 200,000 pending eviction cases that predate COVID-19, and no sign of a new COVID-19 relief bill. This could cause a new surge in homelessness in 2021.
